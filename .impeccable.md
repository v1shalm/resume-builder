## Design Context

### Users
Job-hunting professionals — product designers, engineers, marketers, early-career and senior alike — who want a résumé editor that feels like a tool rather than a template gallery. Used in short bursts (updating a line for a new application) and longer sessions (restructuring sections, rewriting bullets). The exported PDF is the artifact that lands in a recruiter's inbox or ATS; the builder itself stays out of view.

### Brand Personality
Tool-like, efficient, keyboard-first, quiet. Linear / Raycast / Arc adjacent. Zero marketing energy. No "productivity app" clichés. The builder disappears so the work shows through.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Theme**: Dark primary, light available. Standard for this tool category (Linear, Raycast, Figma sidebars) and long editing sessions are common.
- **Type**: Geist Sans + Geist Mono. Mono reserved for keyboard hints, section counts, and metadata — never body.
- **Labels**: Sentence case across the app — form labels, section labels, menu groups, badges. No uppercase micro-labels, no tracked-out mono (Linear/Raycast-adjacent voice, not vintage-manual). Hierarchy comes from weight, size, and color tone — not typographic affectation.
- **Color**: Warm-neutral dark palette tinted slightly toward cool-blue to read as "tool." Single restrained accent (muted amber) reserved for primary save/export actions — the 10% in 60-30-10.
- **Density**: Medium. Tighter than marketing, looser than a code editor. Rhythm through spacing variation, not borders.
- **Preview**: The resume preview pane is a separate visual world — warm paper (not pure white), navy heading accent, sans-only body. Designed to match what the exported PDF becomes.
- **Templates & variants**: Templates are *starting points*, not visual themes — they shape structure and seed content (sections, bullets, ordering). The chrome, typography, and accents stay unified across every template so the gallery never reads as a "pick your look" grid. Variants are a user's own collection of resumes for different roles, not a showcase.
- **Mobile**: Graceful degradation. The editor works on phones for tweaks and review, but the primary surface is desktop. Don't amputate features, but don't redesign for mobile-first either — fix overflow, enlarge touch targets, respect safe areas, and let the hierarchy simplify naturally.
- **Final Review cluster**: The Topbar "Final Review" group (ATS Match and its peers) is a pre-flight checklist — the moment before export where quality checks come into view. Treat it as a distinct micro-mode, not ambient signals scattered through the editor.
- **Anti-references**: Notion (too soft), Typeform (too conversational), generic resume builders (too templated — templates as a *gallery of looks* is the specific failure mode we avoid), Canva (too decorative), any "AI productivity" glow-on-dark aesthetic.

### Design Principles
1. **The tool disappears.** Editor chrome is quiet so the resume preview is the focal point.
2. **One kind of surface.** Flat panels with 1px dividers. No nested cards, no drop shadows as decoration.
3. **Keyboard is a first-class input.** Tab order, shortcuts for save/export/undo, visible shortcut hints in mono. Desktop is the keyboard surface; mobile is the thumb-reachable fallback.
4. **The PDF is the product.** Every rendering decision in the preview must match the exported PDF exactly — no surprise on download.
5. **Restraint over decoration.** No gradients, no glows, no icon walls. Hierarchy comes from type weight and spacing.
6. **Templates are scaffolding, not skins.** A template gives you a head start on structure; the look stays the tool's look.
7. **Final Review is a pre-flight, not a mode toggle.** Quality signals (ATS, overflow, link check) converge at export-time, not as always-on chrome that competes with editing.
